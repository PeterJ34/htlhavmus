4000 BC ~ Thames Track

Thames track, which follows the route that is now the A13, is the earliest track through Essex. Archaeological evidence of the oldest settlements of modern humans has been found along this path.

1592 ~ Francis Quarles

Francis Quarles, born at Stewards, Romford, became a Royalist poet. His most famous work was Emblems (1635), together with Feast of Wormes (1620) and Argalus and Parthenia (1629). Latterly he wrote tracts in defence of Charles I, consequently a great deal of his output was destroyed by Parliamentarians during the Civil Wars. In 1618 he married Ursula Woodgate, they had 18 children. The Quarles family of Romford, divided by the Civil War: Francis Quarles (1592–1644) of Stewards Manor in Romford was poet and Protestant patriot. The Puritans loved his poetry and the Royalists his political pamphlets. His son, Lieutenant Colonel James Quarles, was the most senior officer in Denzil Holles' Parliamentarian regiment at the Battle of Brentford, where he was killed in 1642. Another son, John Quarles (1625–1665), was studying in Oxford when the town became the King's headquarters. He is believed to have served in one of the Royalist companies there in 1642.
